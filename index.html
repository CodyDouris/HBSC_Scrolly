Our world is getting digital by the minute and social media is more prevalent now than ever - but how often do we think of it in terms of youth health?

One study that provides us with information on social media use and the health of young people is the Health Behaviour in School-aged Children (HBSC) Study. The Health Behaviour in School-aged Children (HBSC) study is a World Health Organization collaborative research study of youth aged 11 to 15 that is conducted every 4 years. There are currently over 50 participating countries across Europe and North America.

The HBSC looks at social media use in terms of intensive use and problematic use1

Intensive Social Media Use

Intensive use is based on the frequency of social media use.

Students were asked about how often they have online contact with:

  • Friends from a larger friend group
  • People other than friends, e.g. parents, brothers/sisters, classmates, teachers
  • Friends that they got to know through the internet, but didn’t know before
  • Close friend(s)

Students who answered that they have online contact with others “Almost all the time throughout the day” were considered intensive social media users.

Problematic Social Media Use

Problematic use is based on addiction-like symptoms related to social media use.

Students were asked 9 questions about their use that included:

  • Neglecting other activities
  • Being unable to focus on other things
  • Using social media to escaper from negative feelings
  • Lying about time spent on social media

Students who reported having 6 or more symptoms were considered problematic social media users, 2 to 5 symptoms were considered at moderate risk for problematic use and 0 to 1 symptoms was considered low risk of problematic use.

Figure 1: Percentages (95% confidence intervals) of students who reported intensive social media use by age and gender.

Overall, of the students surveyed aged 11-15, 30.0% of boys and 38.0% of girls reported intensive social media use.

Boy and girls reported similar perspectives of intensive social media use at age 11 (21.0% and 22.7% respectively) but for age 12, only 23.5% of boys surveyed reported intensive social media use, compared to 32.4% of girls.

Across all ages surveyed (11-15) a higher percentage of girls than boys reported intensive social media use but intensive social media use increases steadily from ages 11-15 in both boys and girls.

The gap between percentage of boys and girls reporting intensive social media use also increases with age.

In the 15 year-old demographic, 37.5% of boys reported intensive social media use compared to 45.4% of girls.

A lot of youth use a lot of social media and some people wonder if it might have impacts on their mental health.

In order to understand this better, we looked at the relationship between social media use and mental health (psychological symptoms , emotional problems and life satisfaction.

We saw that across all grades and genders:

Students who were classified as having problematic social media use and at moderate risk of problematic use were more likely to report high psychological symptoms, high emotional problems, and less likely to report high life satisfaction, compared to students at low risk of problematic social media use.

Students are spending significant amount of time online, even if they don’t classify as having problematic use. Boys are more likely than girls to be playing games online, while girls are more likely than boys to be doing homework, social networking or surfing the web.

To look at the relationship between social media use and youth health, HBSC also asked students about their relationships and connections.

Strong relationships and connections are known to be protective to health for young people as they grow and develop through their adolescent years.2,3

National and international HBSC research has identified social media use as an emerging youth health priority

In general, we found that intensive social media use was not associated with the qualities of the connections and relationships reported by young people.

However, we did see that intensive social media use was associated with higher perceived levels of support from friends.

When it came to problematic social media use, we saw that problematic use was generally associated with weaker connections and relationships among both boys and girls.

Social media is an important part in the lives of many adolescents. The impact of the COVID-19 pandemic has caused a lot to shift to the virtual world, leaving social media’s role as a form of connection more prevalent now than before.

Gaining insight into the relationship between social media use and youth health can help us support healthy social media use among young people and prevent potential negative impacts so that they can thrive in this digital world.

Connections
The HBSC measured connections by asking students to rate the importance of connections within themselves (self), to other people (others), to nature and the land (nature), or to something greater (the transcendent) 2
Relationships
Relationships were measured by the strength of supports available from four social contexts - families, teachers, classmates & friends

The HBSC showed us:

  • More girls than boys report intensive (frequent) and problematic (addition-like) social media use
  • Problematic social media use is associated with weaker relationships and connections and worse mental health, but intensive use is not
  • There is an association between mental health and problematic social media use

Internationally, intensive and problematic social media use was reported among 11, 13 and 15 year olds.

Compared to over 40 other countries:

  • Canada had a lower percentage of 11 and 13 year olds reporting intensive and problematic social media use
  • Canada had a higher percentage of 15 year olds reporting intensive social media use versus problematic use
